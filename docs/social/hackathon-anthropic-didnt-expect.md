## LinkedIn

An electrician, a doctor, and a carpenter won Anthropic's latest hackathon. Not the engineers.

That detail comes from Casey Newton's Platformer interview with Boris Cherny, the creator of Claude Code. Most coverage pulled the "coding is solved" quote and moved on. The hackathon line is the one worth sitting with: the people getting the most out of these tools are not the people anyone expected.

The AI-jobs discourse is fighting over one question: will engineers be replaced. Cherny's actual answer is mild. Titles change, the role expands, the number of people writing code goes up a hundred-fold. The better question is the one Newton was circling: when the syntax gate falls, who walks through?

I've watched three answers up close.

I finished my Grade 12 coursework as an adult learner through Sagamok Anishnawbek's Lifelong Learning Centre, with AI in the loop. Not the demographic anyone meant by "AI for developers." The tools worked anyway.

I started a petition asking the Rainbow District School Board for a real public AI policy. Students are using these tools every day. The adults setting the rules haven't caught up.

I'm building Anokii, the embedded chat on oiatc.ca. It isn't built for engineers. It's built so a community member can find a resource and get an answer that cites where it came from.

None of these are "engineers replaced." All of them are "someone got access to a tool that wasn't available before, and the systems around them haven't caught up."
